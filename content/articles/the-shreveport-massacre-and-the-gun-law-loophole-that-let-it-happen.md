Their names were Jayla, Shayla, Kayla, Layla, Markaydon, Sariahh, Khedarrion, and Braylon. They were between the ages of 3 and 11. Most of them were asleep when they were killed. And the man who killed them — the father of seven of them — had a prior gun charge near a school that should have cost him his right to own a weapon. It didn't. And that is not a tragedy. It is a policy choice.

On April 19, 2026, Shreveport, Louisiana became the site of the deadliest mass shooting in the United States since January 2024. Eight children lost their lives. Two mothers were shot in the head. And the paper trail that led to this moment goes back seven years — to a plea deal that closed one door and left another one wide open.

This is the breakdown of what happened, how the system failed, and why we have to stop calling this inevitable.

## What Happened in Cedar Grove — The Facts on the Ground

[On the morning of April 19, 2026, Shamar Elkins, 31, shot and killed eight children at two homes in the Cedar Grove neighborhood of Shreveport, Louisiana](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/20/us/shreveport-louisiana-shooting-what-we-know-hnk). Seven of the children were his own. The eighth was his nephew. The children ranged in age from 3 to 11 years old.

Before the killings, Elkins shot his wife Shaneiqua Pugh at a home on Harrison Street. He then drove to the home of Christina Snow, the mother of three of his other children, where the eight children were sleeping, and opened fire. Police say the children were shot execution style — most while still asleep.

A 12-year-old girl survived by jumping from the roof of the home, sustaining broken bones. One of the two mothers shot in the head was able to use Siri to call 911. Elkins fled the scene, carjacked a vehicle, and was killed during a confrontation with police in neighboring Bossier Parish. According to a preliminary autopsy report, he died from a self-inflicted gunshot wound.

[A relative told CNN that Elkins and his wife were in the process of separating and were due in court the very next day — April 20 — to sign divorce papers.](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/20/us/shreveport-louisiana-shooting-what-we-know-hnk) His brother-in-law said Elkins had told him: "Bro, I don't want to lose my wife."

## Say Their Names — Because America Has Normalized This

Before we go any further into the policy breakdown, we have to stop and say their names. Because we have become a society that normalizes gun violence, mass shootings, and the burying of children — and moves on before the funerals are even over.

Five girls. Three boys. Their whole lives ahead of them. Gone before sunrise on a Sunday morning in April.

The next time you hear someone named Kayla, remember her. The next time you see a child playing in a yard, remember them. These were real children who deserved to become adults — to struggle, to grow, to live. We owe them more than a news cycle.

## The Loophole: He Had a Prior Gun Charge and Got to Keep His Guns Anyway

Here is where this story stops being about one man's violence and becomes about a system that handed him the tools to commit it.

[In March 2019, Shamar Elkins was arrested on two charges: illegal use of weapons and carrying a firearm on school property](https://newrepublic.com/post/209277/louisiana-shooter-able-get-gun-prior-convictions). He had fired five rounds from a handgun approximately 300 feet from the fence line of Caddo Magnet High School in Shreveport. Children were playing outside the school when those rounds were fired.

The more serious charge — carrying a firearm on school property, a felony punishable by up to five years in prison — was dismissed as part of a plea deal. Elkins pleaded guilty to the lesser charge of illegal use of weapons and received 18 months of supervised probation.

[When his probation ended in 2021, Shamar Elkins was legally free to own a firearm](https://www.inkl.com/news/shamar-elkins-fatal-loophole-how-a-2019-probation-deal-for-school-zone-gunfire-left-the-army-veteran-armed). Under Louisiana's gun possession statute, only certain felonies — crimes of violence, sex crimes, drug crimes — trigger the state's 10-year bar on firearm possession. The offense Elkins pleaded guilty to sat below that threshold.

Let that sink in. A man fired a gun near a school where children were playing outside. He took a plea deal. He served probation. And five years later, he was legally permitted to own the weapon he used to murder eight children — most of them his own.

This is not a monster story. This is a system story. And the system has a paper trail.

## The Weapon — Another Layer of Failure

[On April 21, authorities arrested 56-year-old Charles Ford, a convicted felon, in connection with the rifle-style pistol Elkins used in the shooting](https://www.ksla.com/2026/04/21/shreveport-man-arrested-allegedly-securing-gun-shamar-elkins-used-mass-shooting-that-left-8-kids-dead/). Ford was charged with felon in possession of a firearm and making false statements to federal agents. Investigators say Ford initially denied having the gun, then admitted he had possessed it and believed Elkins had stolen it from his truck.

This means the weapon used to kill eight children passed through the hands of at least one convicted felon before reaching Elkins. The ATF and FBI are both investigating. And yet the answer to how Elkins obtained a weapon may ultimately have been perfectly legal — because the system said he was allowed to have one.

That is the point. The loophole isn't just a technicality. It is a door that the law built, propped open, and left unguarded.

## This Is a Domestic Violence Story — And America Keeps Treating It as a Private Matter

When we say "mass shooting," most people picture a stranger. A grudge. A public place. But the deadliest shooter in American homes is almost always already in the house.

[Roughly 60% of mass shootings in America are rooted in domestic or family violence](https://lailluminator.com/2026/04/24/shreveport-mass-shooting-2/). Elkins fits squarely inside that majority. This was not a random act of violence by an unknown threat. This was a man who felt his control slipping — a separation, divorce papers due the next morning, a confrontation over a marriage ending — and responded with lethal force against the people he claimed to love.

The presence of a gun in a home where domestic violence exists increases the risk of homicide by 500%. This is documented. This is researched. This is why so many people in abusive situations quietly insist there be no gun in the house — because they understand, often from experience, what that statistic means.

Police in Shreveport said they had no prior record of domestic violence calls involving Elkins. But this is not surprising — and it is not exculpatory. Domestic violence is the most underreported violent crime in America. There are a hundred reasons why someone doesn't call the police: distrust of law enforcement, fear of escalation, economic dependency, shame, and the very real possibility that calling for help will make things worse before it makes them better.

No prior calls does not mean no prior violence. It means the violence was private. Hidden. Normalized inside the home in ways the neighbors couldn't see, couldn't hear, and couldn't intervene in.

## Familicide — When Control Becomes the Motive for Mass Murder

Sociologists call what Elkins did familicide — when a perpetrator kills his family, often as an act of ultimate control in a moment when that control is perceived to be slipping away.

The pattern is well-documented. A separation. A custody dispute. A confrontation. A man who feels he is losing his grip on the people he has defined himself through having. Research consistently shows that the most dangerous period in a domestic violence situation is the moment of separation or attempted separation — when the abuser feels the relationship is ending and their dominance is being challenged.

Elkins had told his brother-in-law he didn't want to lose his wife. Divorce papers were due in court the morning after the killings. The timing is not a coincidence. It is the pattern.

Perpetrators of familicide often present publicly as loving parents — because the violence is not about hatred of the children. It is about the ultimate act of possession. If you are going to leave me, you are not taking what I consider mine. Children, in the mind of a man like this, are not people with their own lives and futures. They are possessions. Extensions of control. And when that control is threatened, the violence turns lethal.

This is why it is critically important — for every parent, every family member, every person in a relationship — to understand the difference between a partner who loves you and a partner who controls you. Controlling behavior is not love. And it does not stay at the level of pinches and criticism. It graduates.

## Mike Johnson Called It a Tragedy — But His Voting Record Helped Create It

House Speaker Mike Johnson, whose congressional district includes Shreveport, called the shooting a "heartbreaking tragedy" and said he was holding the victims' families in his thoughts and prayers.

Mike Johnson has consistently opposed gun control legislation in Congress.

Those two things cannot coexist without accountability. You cannot call a preventable massacre heartbreaking while actively working to keep the policy conditions that made it possible in place. This is not political point-scoring. It is the sociological imagination — the ability to connect personal troubles to public failures.

Sociologist C. Wright Mills defined the sociological imagination as the capacity to see how individual experiences connect to larger social structures. Eight dead children in Shreveport is not a personal tragedy that fell from the sky. It is a public failure with a paper trail that goes back to a 2019 plea deal, a Louisiana gun statute with a threshold too low to stop a man who fired near a school from legally obtaining another weapon five years later.

That paper trail has names on it. The children's names are the ones we say. But the names of the legislators who have blocked, watered down, and defunded gun control measures for decades belong in the record too.

## The Numbers That Put This in Context

This was [the deadliest mass shooting in the United States since January 2024, and the deadliest single-day mass shooting in Louisiana history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Shreveport_shooting). According to the Gun Violence Archive, there had already been six incidents of mass murder in the United States in 2026 before April 19th.

Six mass murders before April 19th of a single year. In no other developed country do these numbers exist at this frequency. The variable is not mental illness — other countries have people with mental illness. The variable is access. And access is a policy decision.

The Giffords Law Center — founded by former congresswoman Gabby Giffords, herself a survivor of gun violence — called this an act of domestic gun violence, framing it explicitly as a policy failure rather than an isolated act of individual pathology. That framing is correct. Because when you can trace the weapon, the legal loophole, and the missed intervention points in a straight line from 2019 to April 19, 2026, you are not looking at an unavoidable tragedy. You are looking at a preventable one.

## How Many More Children Before We Call This What It Is?

This is not a question about hunting rights. This is not a question about whether law-abiding citizens should be able to own firearms. This is a question about whether a man who fired a gun near a school where children were playing should have been legally permitted to own another one five years later.

The answer the system gave was yes. Eight children paid for that answer with their lives.

We write laws in this country. We vote for people who make those laws. We have the capacity to close loopholes that allow people with prior gun charges near schools to legally rearm themselves. We have the capacity to treat domestic violence as the predictive precursor to mass killing that researchers have documented it to be. We have the capacity to require that when someone fires a weapon near a school — near children — the system does not just hand them probation and send them home.

We have not done these things. And the question that follows is not complicated: how many more children do we have to bury before we stop calling this a tragedy and start calling it what it actually is — a policy choice?
